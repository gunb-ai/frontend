Gunbai · Design Reference · v4

Design decisions, grounded

This page captures why the design looks the way it does—not just the tokens. If a future decision contradicts something here, either the decision is wrong or this document needs updating. Both are fine. Drifting silently is not.

Design philosophy

Down to earth

Gunbai is a structural compiler. It enforces invariants, fails closed, names its boundaries honestly. The design should do the same: state what it is, acknowledge what it isn't, and never perform excitement it doesn't feel.

"Down to earth" is the unifying idea. The colors are not UI conventions (green = success, red = danger). They are materials: moss grew there slowly; clay is where the ground cracked. The palette tells you the compiler is grounded, not reactive. Earthy does not mean rustic—it means honest about what things are made of.

The aesthetic is quiet, not minimal. There is substance—structure, density, information—but it doesn't compete for attention. A compiler that works correctly doesn't need to announce that it worked. A compiler that hit a boundary doesn't need to scream.

Themes

Six words, three tensions

These themes pull in different directions on purpose. Each pair resolves to a single idea—not a compromise, but a position.

Quiet ↔ Strong
Quiet power

Strength through mass and stillness, not volume. The compiler enforces invariants silently. When correctness holds, the signal is absence—nothing went wrong. Power you infer from what didn't happen.

Awkward ↔ Cute
Earnest charm

A compiler with personality is inherently awkward. It doesn't try to be charming—the charm comes from being utterly sincere about being a compiler. Proportional, not performed.

Earthy ↔ Structure
Bonsai

Natural materials in rigid geometry. Warm colors inside hard edges. Living material forced into deliberate form—like the project itself: organic domain logic shaped by structural types and graph invariants.

Constraints

Things the design must survive

These are not preferences—they are hard requirements that eliminate options. Any direction that fails one of these is wrong regardless of how good it looks.

  • Mode Light and dark. The palette must produce good contrast in both. Product UI is dark-first (compiler context). Marketing/docs may be either. Colors cannot be tuned for one and awkward in the other.
  • Size 16px favicon. The mark must be recognizable at 16×16. This eliminates detail, thin strokes, and anything that depends on color to read. A bookmark bar is the harshest test.
  • Unity One mark everywhere. Favicon, GitHub avatar, docs site, CLI output, future app icon. No "marketing logo" vs "product icon" drift. Same shape, same read, every context.
  • Type Monospace is native. This is a compiler. Mono isn't a style choice—it's the actual register of the product. Serif is for the slow, permanent layer (manifesto, section titles). No display sans.
  • Format CSS/SVG achievable. The mark shouldn't require raster assets at any size. If it can't be drawn in a viewBox, it's too complex.
  • Tone No saturation spikes. The palette is earthy. Nothing should read as a UI alert. Moss is not "success green." Clay is not "error red." They are materials, not traffic lights.
  • System Colors are parametric. Moss, clay, stone, and the neutral stack should be derivable from shared parameters (hue family, saturation band, brightness curve). If you can't express the palette as a function, the colors are ad hoc and will drift independently.
Palette

Materials, not signals

Every color has a material name and a reason. The reasoning matters more than the hex—if a color drifts from its reason, re-derive the hex from the reason, don't update the reason to match the hex.

Parametric principle: moss and clay should live at matched saturation and lightness—they're the same kind of material (earth pigment) in different hue families. Adjusting one should pull the other. The neutrals (stone, void, warm white) share a warm undertone derived from the same base temperature. Future tooling should express these as functions, not independent hex values.

Void
hsl(37, 0%, 5%)
The ground. Near-black, very slightly warm. Product surfaces, the compiler's natural habitat.
Moss
hsl(134, 36%, 48%)
Quiet confidence. Correctness held. Things grew here slowly. Links, active paths, success that doesn't celebrate.
Clay
hsl(7, 36%, 48%)
Calm boundary. Something cracked, but nothing exploded. The compiler reports, it doesn't react. Errors, warnings, invariant violations.
Stone
hsl(37, 15%, 73%)
Primary text on dark. Warm grey—not blue-tinted, not cool. Readable, recessive, patient.
Warm White
hsl(37, 27%, 94%)
Light mode surface. Off-white with a warm cast. Not clinical, not paper—somewhere between.

Why not saturated? A saturated green says "go." A saturated red says "stop." Those are instructions. The compiler doesn't instruct—it reports state. Moss says "this held." Clay says "this cracked." The user decides what to do.

Typography

One voice, two registers

Mono is the native voice. CLI, pipeline stages, code, labels, body text. This is a compiler—monospace is not an affectation, it's the actual medium.

Display is the same voice, loosened. Recursive at half-mono with a touch of casual—headlines, manifesto, section titles. It shares DNA with the body mono so the page reads as one system, not two bolted-together typefaces.

No serif. No display sans. One family of forms, dialed wider for hierarchy.

Mark

The Stoic Giant

A deadpan face in a squircle. Two round eyes, one flat mouth—the minimum geometry for expression. Eyes and mouth are transparent cutouts (evenodd fill), so the background always shows through. This gives infinite palette flexibility from a single SVG path.

Team palettes. Every team member gets a personal background + face color pair. The canonical product mark uses stone-on-void (dark) and text-on-cream (light). Accent variants in moss and clay work for branded contexts. See palette grid →

Voice

Says what it does, names what it doesn't

State facts. "Compilation halted." Not "Oops!" Not "Critical failure." The compiler encountered a boundary. It stopped. That's what it's supposed to do.

Name tradeoffs. If something costs complexity, say so. If a use case is excluded, say that too. Bonsai is honest pruning—say what was cut and why.

Don't sell. No "empower your team," no "blazingly fast," no unqualified absolutes. If the math is there, show the math. If it's not, say "designed to" or "attempts" instead of "guarantees."

Charm is structural. Don't force whimsy. The personality comes from proportion and sincerity, not from trying to be funny. A compiler that takes itself exactly as seriously as it should is inherently a little endearing.

Errors are weather. Clay appears. The body stays still. Something cracked in the ground—the compiler reports it the way a geologist would: precisely, calmly, with coordinates.

Diagrams

DAG rendering — future work

The landing page should eventually include a holistic DAG diagram—something that communicates the compiler's worldview at a glance: workflows as directed graphs, structure as node + edge composition, the pipeline as a left-to-right chain of pure transforms. Think agent workflow visualizations, but grounded in the compiler's actual semantics.

Style constraint: the diagram must be consistent with the rest of the page's text-first, monospace-native register. The initial moodboard used ASCII box-drawing characters for branch diagrams (○──○──○, │, └──). Any richer rendering (SVG, canvas) should feel like a natural extension of that vocabulary—not a foreign element dropped into the page.

Source material: gunbc/docs/architecture.md describes the substrate (Node + Edge), the two structural patterns (Product = all edges connected, Coproduct = one edge active), the pipeline stages (parse → resolve → typecheck → lower → emit), and the composition stack (classical logic → bit → machine words → algebraic types → collections → domain). The README has the pipeline diagram and the v2 compiler file layout. These are the reference for what to draw.

Open direction: could be ASCII/text-only (most on-brand), a minimal CSS/HTML rendering using the existing panel/border vocabulary, or a carefully constrained SVG that inherits the page's type and color system. Dedicated session.

Resolved

Decisions made in this version

Parametric color system. Implemented. Moss and clay now share saturation (36%) and lightness (48% base, 36% dim) derived from three hue families: warm (37°), moss (134°), clay (7°). The neutral stack derives from the warm hue with saturation that rises naturally with lightness. Adjusting --accent-sat pulls both accents consistently.

Mark shape. Resolved. The Stoic Giant — a deadpan face in a squircle — replaces the neutral placeholder. Evenodd cutouts keep it single-path and infinitely recolorable. Reads at 16px. Each team member gets a personal color pair.

Mascot / motion. The Stoic Giant doubles as the mascot. Idle breathing + blink animations are planned for the website (CSS/GSAP). Sleeping state available for backgrounded tabs.

Open questions

Decisions not yet made

1. Mark shape. Resolved. The Stoic Giant. See mark section above.

2. The tree / DAG motif. The project's data structure is a DAG. A procedural branching tree maps both literally (compiler structure) and thematically (bonsai = organic material in deliberate form). Open question: does this become the hero visual for the landing page, a background texture, or something else? The diagram section above establishes the structural vocabulary; the tree would be its expressive form.

3. Landing page scope. The page needs to communicate: this exists, these are its goals/non-goals, try it here, reach out here. What's the minimum viable structure? Hero + pipeline diagram + install + contact? Or something leaner?
